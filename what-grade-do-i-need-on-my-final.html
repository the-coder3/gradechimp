What Grade Do I Need on My Final Exam?

5 min read · Updated April 2026

It's the night before finals and you're asking yourself: "What do I need on my final to pass?" or "Can I still get an A?" You're not alone — millions of students ask this exact question every semester.

The good news is that figuring out what you need on your final exam is simple math. You only need three numbers: your current grade, your final exam's weight, and the grade you want. Here's how to do it.

The Formula

Here's the formula to calculate what grade you need on your final exam:

Final Exam Score = (Target Grade − Current Grade × (1 − Final Weight)) ÷ Final Weight

Don't worry if that looks confusing. Let's break it down with real examples.

Example 1: "I Have an 85% and Want an A"

📝 Example

Current grade: 85%

Final exam weight: 30%

Target grade: 90% (A-)

Calculation: (90 − 85 × 0.70) ÷ 0.30 = (90 − 59.5) ÷ 0.30 = 101.7%

Bad news — you'd need over 100% on the final, which is impossible without extra credit. You might want to aim for a B+ instead.

Example 2: "I Have a 72% and Just Want to Pass"

📝 Example

Current grade: 72%

Final exam weight: 40%

Target grade: 70% (C-)

Calculation: (70 − 72 × 0.60) ÷ 0.40 = (70 − 43.2) ÷ 0.40 = 67%

You need a 67% on the final to pass with a C-. That's very doable — even a below-average performance will get you there.

Example 3: "I Have a 94% and Want to Keep My A"

📝 Example

Current grade: 94%

Final exam weight: 25%

Target grade: 93% (A)

Calculation: (93 − 94 × 0.75) ÷ 0.25 = (93 − 70.5) ÷ 0.25 = 90%

You need a 90% on the final to keep your A. Challenging but realistic — study hard and you've got this.

5 Tips to Get the Grade You Need

1. Start studying now, not the night before. Spreading your study sessions across 3-5 days is proven to improve retention compared to cramming.

2. Focus on practice problems, not re-reading notes. Testing yourself (active recall) is 3x more effective than passively reviewing material.

3. Study the high-weight topics first. Check your syllabus — if Chapter 7 is 30% of the final, that's where your time should go.

4. Ask your teacher what's on the exam. Most teachers will tell you which chapters or topics to focus on if you ask. It's not cheating — it's being smart.

5. Sleep 7-8 hours the night before. Your brain consolidates memories during sleep. An all-nighter does more harm than good.

What If I Need Over 100%?

If the calculator shows you need more than 100% on your final, here are your options:

  1. Ask about extra credit. Many professors offer extra credit opportunities, especially at the end of the semester. It never hurts to ask.
  2. Check if there's a grade curve. Some classes curve final grades, which could bump you up.
  3. See if you can redo any assignments. Some teachers allow re-submissions on homework or projects.
  4. Adjust your target. If an A is impossible, aim for a B+ instead. Getting the highest grade you can still matters.
  5. Talk to your teacher or advisor. They've seen students in your situation before and might have suggestions you haven't thought of.

How Does the Final Exam Weight Work?

Your final exam weight is the percentage of your total grade that the final exam counts for. You can find this on your syllabus. Common weights are 20%, 25%, 30%, or 40%.

The higher the weight, the more power your final exam has to change your grade — for better or worse. A final worth 40% can swing your grade dramatically, while a final worth 15% won't move it much either way.

For example, if your final is worth 40% and you currently have a 75%, getting a 95% on the final would bring your overall grade up to 83% (B). But if the final is only worth 20%, that same 95% would only bring you up to 79% (C+).

Frequently Asked Questions

What if I don't know my current grade?

Check your school's online gradebook (Canvas, Blackboard, PowerSchool, etc.) or ask your teacher. If you can't find it, use our Grade Average Calculator to calculate it from your individual assignment scores.

Does this work for college and high school?

Yes. The formula is the same regardless of whether you're in middle school, high school, or college. The only thing that changes is the grading scale your school uses.

What if my class uses total points instead of percentages?

Convert your points to a percentage first. Divide your earned points by the total possible points and multiply by 100. For example, 450 out of 600 points = 75%.

Can I calculate this for multiple classes?

Yes — just use the calculator once for each class. You can also use our GPA Calculator to see how all your classes combine into your overall GPA.
